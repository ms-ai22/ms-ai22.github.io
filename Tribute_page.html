Srinivasa Ramanujan The man who knew infinity



  • 1887 - Born on 22 December in Erode, India
  • 1897 - First encounter with formal mathematics on entry into town higher secondary school
  • 1904 - At the age of 17, he calculated the Euler–Mascheroni constant up to 15 decimal places
  • 1910 - Inclusion as a researcher at the University of Madras, after meeting the founder of the Indian Mathematical Society
  • 1910 - With help from Ramasamy Aiyer and financial aid from Ramachandra Rao, he published his first work in the journal of the Indian Mathematical Society
  • 1913 - With the help of friends, Ramanujan drafted letters along with his papers to leading mathematicians at Cambridge University. Hardy was impressed and invited Ramanujan to London, to share the proofs for the assertions
  • 1914 - Ramanujan moves to London. Hardy had already received 120 theorems from Ramanujan in the first two letters, but there were many more results and theorems in the notebooks. There were new breakthroughs
  • 1916 - He was awarded a Bachelor of Arts by Research degree for his work on highly composite numbeers, sections of the first part of which had been published the preceding year in the Proceedings of the London Mathematical Society
  • 1917 - Ramanujan was elected to the London Mathematical Society
  • 1918 - In May, at age 31, he was one of the youngest Fellows in the Royal Society's history and only the second Indian admitted after Ardaseer Cursetjee in 1841. He was elected "for his investigation in elliptic functions and the Theory of Numbers"
  • 1918 - In October, He was the first Indian to be elected a Fellow of Trinity College, Cambridge
  • 1919 - Ramanujan was plagued by health problems throughout his life. Owing to the difficulty of keeping to the strict dietary requirements of his religion in England and because of wartime rationing in 1914–18, he was diagnosed with tuberculosis and a severe vitamin deficiency and confined to a sanatorium. He returned to Kumbakonam, India
  • 1919 - He died at the age of 32. Before his death, he discovered mock theta functions. After his death, his brother Tirunarayanan compiled Ramanujan's remaining handwritten notes, consisting of formulae on singular moduli, hypergeometric series and continued fractions

Ramanujan was a rigorously orthodox Hindu and lived a simple life in Cambridge.
He credited his acumen to his family goddess, Namagiri Thayar (Goddess Mahalakshmi) of Namakkal.
He looked to her for inspiration in his work
An equation for me has no meaning unless it expresses a thought of God

Hardy and Ramanujan had highly contrasting personalities.
Their collaboration was a clash of different cultures, beliefs, and working styles.
Hardy and Ramanujan studied the partition function extensively.
Their work in this area gave rise to a powerful new method for finding asymptotic formulae called the circle method.
The number 1729 is known as the Hardy–Ramanujan number after a famous visit by Hardy

"Never met his equal and can compare him only with Euler and Jacobi"

- G. H. Hardy (Mathematician and friend)
